Grade 10 History · Apartheid legislation

The Group Areas Act

How the apartheid state used a single 1950 law to divide South Africa's cities by race and to force millions of people from their homes.

Subject: History Grade 10 · FET Phase CAPS: Apartheid in the 1950s Duration: ~30 min

South African History Online · Classroom. Prepared as an open educational resource.

Speaker notes

Open by asking learners what they already know about where people were "allowed" to live under apartheid. Establish that this lesson looks at one law that reshaped every South African city.

Flag the two big ideas we will build toward: (1) segregation was made legal and bureaucratic, and (2) it had a deeply human cost measured in destroyed communities.

Setting the scene

Apartheid was built law by law

After the National Party won the 1948 election, it turned racial segregation into a formal legal system called apartheid ("apartness").

A cluster of laws passed in the early 1950s worked together:

  • The Population Registration Act (1950) classified every person into a racial group.
  • The Group Areas Act (1950) decided where each group could live and trade.
  • Later removals were enforced under the Natives Resettlement Act (1954) and pass laws.

Key term

Segregation keeping racial groups physically apart in housing, schools and public space, backed by the power of the state.

Speaker notes

Stress the "law by law" idea: no single act created apartheid; it was an interlocking system. The Group Areas Act only worked because the Population Registration Act had already labelled everyone.

Check understanding of "segregation" before moving on - ask for an example from learners' own town or city.

The law itself · 1950

What the Group Areas Act actually did

1. Divided the map

It let the government declare any urban area as reserved for a single racial group.

2. Controlled ownership

People could only own or occupy property in the area assigned to their group.

3. Forced removals

Anyone in the "wrong" area could be ordered to sell up and move out.

4. Punished non-compliance

Staying put became a criminal offence, enforced by police and demolition.

In short: the state gave itself the power to redraw the racial geography of every town and city in the country.

Speaker notes

Walk the four cards in order - they escalate from "drawing lines" to "criminalising your own home". Emphasise that removal was not a side effect; it was the mechanism.

Ask: whose land and businesses tended to be declared for other groups? (Black, Coloured and Indian communities in valuable, central locations.)

Chronology

From statute to bulldozer

Speaker notes

Point out the gap between 1950 (the law) and 1968-82 (mass removals in places like District Six). Injustice was slow and administrative, which is part of what made it so total.

1991 repeal: note it took until the very end of apartheid to remove this law - a good hook for a later lesson on the transition.

Case study · Cape Town

District Six: a community erased

District Six was a lively, mixed neighbourhood near central Cape Town, home to about 60,000 people.

  • Declared a whites-only area in 1966.
  • Residents were forcibly moved to the distant Cape Flats.
  • Homes, shops and places of worship were bulldozed.
  • Little was ever rebuilt on the cleared land.

Speaker notes

District Six is the classic teaching case because so much testimony survives. Emphasise distance: the Cape Flats move meant longer, costlier commutes and broken community networks.

Optional link: mention the District Six Museum as a place where this memory is preserved.

Human impact

What removal felt like

“They took our houses, our streets, our neighbours - a whole world that could never be put back together in the same way.” Composite of District Six residents' testimonies (illustrative, for teaching)

Behind every removal statistic was a family that lost a home, a business, a school route and a community. The Act treated people as units on a map; this is why we study the human cost, not only the legal text.

Speaker notes

This quote is a teaching composite - be explicit with learners that it stands in for many real testimonies. In the production lesson we would cite a specific sourced oral history from the SAHO archive.

Pause here. Let the emotional weight land before moving to resistance and legacy.

Resistance & legacy

People did not simply accept it

Resistance

  • Communities petitioned, protested and went to court.
  • Removals fed anger that powered the wider anti-apartheid struggle.
  • Artists, writers and photographers recorded what was lost.

Legacy today

  • Many South African cities are still shaped by these old racial lines.
  • Land restitution claims continue for removed communities.
  • Museums and memory projects keep the history alive.

Speaker notes

Balance the trauma with agency: people resisted and remembered. Draw the line from 1950s removals to present-day spatial inequality that learners can see around them.

Mention restitution to signal that this is unfinished history, not a closed chapter.

Consolidation

Summary & source-based questions

Three things to remember

  • The Group Areas Act (1950) legalised racial zoning of cities.
  • It powered forced removals like District Six.
  • Its effects still shape South Africa today.

Q1. Explain, in your own words, how the Population Registration Act and the Group Areas Act depended on each other. (4 marks)

Q2. Using the District Six example, describe two ways forced removal harmed a community. (6 marks)
