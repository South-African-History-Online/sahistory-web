Grade 9 History - Senior Phase

Turning Points: 1948 and the 1950s

How the 1948 election put apartheid into law, and how ordinary South Africans began to fight back.

Speaker notes

Open by asking learners what they already know about apartheid. Establish the two threads of this lesson: (1) the laws the National Party passed after winning power in 1948, and (2) the growing resistance to those laws during the 1950s. Explain that a 'turning point' is a moment that changes the direction of history.

Setting the scene

South Africa before 1948

Segregation did not begin in 1948 - but 1948 made it far more organised and total.

Long before 1948, laws already separated South Africans by race and controlled where black people could live, work and move. Pass laws forced black men to carry documents, and the 1913 Natives Land Act reserved most farmland for white owners.

  • Wealth and land were held mostly by the white minority.
  • Black, Coloured and Indian people had few or no voting rights.
  • Segregation was common but not yet a single, complete system.

Speaker notes

Stress continuity and change: segregation existed under earlier governments, but it was patchy. After 1948 the National Party welded these separate rules into one all-covering system called apartheid (an Afrikaans word meaning 'apartness'). Link back to earlier terms on pass laws and land dispossession.

The turning point

The 1948 election

On 26 May 1948 the National Party won power on a promise of apartheid.

The National Party (NP), led by D.F. Malan, campaigned on the slogan of apartheid - keeping racial groups completely separate. In alliance with the Afrikaner Party it defeated Jan Smuts's United Party.

  • Only white voters could take part in the election.
  • The NP won a majority of seats even though it won fewer total votes than the United Party.
  • D.F. Malan became Prime Minister and began turning apartheid into law.

Speaker notes

Highlight why 1948 is THE turning point of this unit: a change of government that deliberately built apartheid. Point out the fairness problem - the NP won seats but not the popular vote, because rural (mostly Afrikaner) constituencies were weighted more heavily. Note that the millions of black, Coloured and Indian South Africans had no say at all.

Building apartheid

Turning apartheid into law

Between 1950 and 1953 the government passed a chain of laws to control every part of life.

Classify

Decide which racial group every person belonged to.

Separate

Force different groups to live, learn and work apart.

Control

Silence and punish anyone who resisted the system.

Speaker notes

Preview the four laws that follow using these three verbs - classify, separate, control. This gives learners a framework so the individual Acts are not just a list to memorise but steps in one plan. Ask learners to predict which law does which job as you move through the next slides.

Classify - Act No. 30 of 1950

Population Registration Act, 1950

Every South African was placed into a racial group, recorded by the state.

This law required people to be classified by race - as White, Coloured or Native (black African). Indian/Asian people were later added as a separate group. A person's classification decided where they could live, work, go to school and whom they could marry.

  • Officials used appearance, descent and social acceptance to classify people.
  • Families could be split when members were classified differently.
  • This Act became the foundation the other apartheid laws were built on.

Speaker notes

Explain that classification was the master key of apartheid: once the state fixed your 'race', every other law could be applied to you. Share that classifications were sometimes arbitrary and cruel - siblings could be placed in different groups. Emphasise the human cost, not just the legal detail.

Separate - Act No. 41 of 1950

Group Areas Act, 1950

The law divided towns and cities into racial zones.

The Group Areas Act let the government reserve any urban area for a single racial group. People living in the 'wrong' area could be forcibly removed from their homes and moved to areas far from city centres and jobs.

  • Whole communities were uprooted and their homes demolished.
  • The best-located land was almost always reserved for white residents.
  • Well-known removals included District Six in Cape Town and Sophiatown in Johannesburg.

Speaker notes

Connect this law to the everyday geography learners may recognise in their own towns - many South African cities still show this apartheid pattern. Sophiatown appears again two slides later as a case study, so plant the seed here. Ask: what does it feel like to be told your home is now in the 'wrong' place?

Control - Act No. 44 of 1950

Suppression of Communism Act, 1950

A law written so broadly it could silence almost any opponent of apartheid.

This Act banned the Communist Party, but it defined 'communism' so widely that it could be used against anyone who opposed government policy. The Minister could 'ban' people - restricting their movement, silencing them and barring them from meetings - without a trial.

  • Activists could be 'listed', banned or banished from their homes.
  • Banning made it a crime for many leaders to speak in public or be quoted.
  • The state used this law throughout the 1950s to weaken resistance.

Speaker notes

This is the 'control' law. Make clear the danger of a vague law: because 'communism' was defined so loosely, the government could silence trade unionists, church leaders and liberals who had nothing to do with communism. Connect to the idea of the rule of law - here punishment came without a fair trial.

Separate - Act No. 47 of 1953

Bantu Education Act, 1953

A deliberately inferior education designed to keep black children in low-paid work.

The government took control of black schooling from the churches and missions that had run many schools. Hendrik Verwoerd, the Minister in charge, made clear the aim: to train black children only for a life of labour and servitude, not equal opportunity.

  • Black schools were given far less money than white schools.
  • The curriculum limited what black learners were taught.
  • Anger at Bantu Education fed resistance for decades, up to 1976.

Speaker notes

Quote or paraphrase Verwoerd's aim carefully at a Grade 9 level: education was used as a tool to limit black children's futures. Draw the long line forward - the 1976 Soweto uprising, which learners meet in the next unit, was sparked by anger at Bantu Education. This shows how a 1950s law had consequences for decades.

Chronology

Apartheid takes shape, 1948-1956

Speaker notes

Use this slide to consolidate before moving from 'the laws' to 'the resistance'. Ask learners to notice the pattern: a burst of apartheid laws in 1950, then a rising wave of protest from 1952 onwards. The rest of the lesson follows that resistance.

Resistance begins - 1952

The Defiance Campaign, 1952

Thousands deliberately broke apartheid laws to fill the jails and show their rejection of the system.

Launched on 26 June 1952 by the African National Congress (ANC) and the South African Indian Congress, volunteers broke unjust laws on purpose - using 'whites-only' facilities and defying curfews - and accepted arrest without violence.

  • It was the first large-scale, multi-racial campaign of non-violent defiance.
  • Leaders included Nelson Mandela, Walter Sisulu and James Moroka.
  • ANC membership grew sharply as the campaign spread across the country.

Speaker notes

Emphasise the strategy: non-violent civil disobedience, inspired partly by earlier passive resistance. The point was moral pressure and mass numbers, not force. Flag for discussion the government's response - the Defiance Campaign led directly to harsher laws and later to the Treason Trial (final slide).

Case study - February 1955

The removal of Sophiatown

A lively, mixed Johannesburg suburb destroyed to enforce the Group Areas Act.

Sophiatown was one of the few places where black South Africans could own land, and it was famous for its music and community life. From 9 February 1955 the government began forcibly removing residents to Meadowlands in Soweto and demolishing their homes.

  • Residents protested with the slogan 'Ons dak nie, ons phola hier' (we are not moving).
  • The area was rebuilt for white residents and renamed 'Triomf' (triumph).
  • Sophiatown became a symbol of what forced removals destroyed.

Speaker notes

Use Sophiatown to make the Group Areas Act concrete and human. Link back to slide 6. Mention its cultural importance (writers, musicians of the Drum era) so learners see removals destroyed not just buildings but a way of life. The renaming to 'Triomf' shows the government celebrated this destruction.

A vision for the future - 1955

The Congress of the People and the Freedom Charter

On 26 June 1955 about 3,000 delegates gathered at Kliptown to say what kind of country they wanted.

The People Shall Govern! South Africa belongs to all who live in it, black and white. The Freedom Charter, Kliptown, 26 June 1955

The Congress of the People was organised by the Congress Alliance. Delegates gathered demands from across the country into the Freedom Charter, a statement of the rights and freedoms people wanted for a democratic South Africa.

  • It demanded equal rights, land, work and education for all.
  • It later shaped the values in South Africa's democratic Constitution.
  • Police broke up the second day of the gathering and recorded those present.

Speaker notes

Contrast this slide with the apartheid laws: here is the alternative vision. Explain the Congress Alliance briefly (ANC and its allies representing different communities). Note the direct line from the Freedom Charter to the 1996 Constitution - a 1950s document that shaped democracy 40 years later. Police surveillance of the crowd links forward to the Treason Trial.

Women lead - 9 August 1956

The Women's March, 1956

About 20,000 women marched on the Union Buildings against pass laws for women.

Organised through the Federation of South African Women (FEDSAW), women of all races marched to the Union Buildings in Pretoria to protest plans to extend the hated pass laws to black women. They left thousands of signed petitions at Prime Minister Strijdom's office.

  • Leaders included Lilian Ngoyi, Helen Joseph, Rahima Moosa and Sophia Williams-De Bruyn.
  • The marchers sang 'Wathint' abafazi, wathint' imbokodo' (you strike a woman, you strike a rock).
  • 9 August is now celebrated every year as National Women's Day.

Speaker notes

Foreground women's leadership - a chance to broaden learners' picture of the struggle beyond famous men. Explain what pass laws were (documents controlling movement) and why extending them to women was so resented. Connect to the present: National Women's Day makes this event part of learners' own calendar.

The state strikes back - and review

The Treason Trial and what we learned

In December 1956 the government arrested 156 leaders and charged them with high treason.

The government accused 156 activists of high treason, claiming the Freedom Charter proved a violent plot to overthrow the state. The trial dragged on for years; in the end all the accused were found not guilty. It showed both the state's determination to crush resistance and the movement's resilience.

Why is 1948 described as a turning point in South African history? (4 marks)

Choose one 1950s law and explain how it separated or controlled people's lives. (6 marks)
